    I am going to assume that linearity of expectation applies to integrals in addition to finite sums.
    \[ E[X(t)]=\int_0^tE\big[W(s)\big]ds=\int_0^t0ds=0 \]
    Because the mean of $X$ is always zero, covariance becomes a simple product:  
    \[ \Cov(X(t),X(s))=E\big[X(t)X(s)\big]=E\Big[\Big(\int_0^t W(s)ds\Big)\Big(\int_0^sW(u)du\Big)\Big] \]
    We can turn this product into a double integral, then distribute the $E$:
    \[ \Cov(X(t),X(s))=\int_0^t\int_0^sE\Big[W(x)W(u)\Big]dxdu \]
    % Fubini's Theorem
    But because Brownian motion $W$ has mean zero, that inner expression is actually the covariance of Brownian motion $r_W$. We already know that $r_W(s,t)=\sigma^2\min\{s,t\}$ for $s,t>0$. This can then be split up into triangles to evaluate $\min$. For the region $s\le t$ we have $x \in [0,t], y \in [0,x]$ and $x \in [0,s], y \in [0,x]$. This gives us
    \[ \Cov(X(t),S(t))=\int_0^t\int_0^x\sigma^2 tdydx+\int_0^s\int_0^x\sigma^2 sdydx\]
    Evaluating these integrals gives the final result:
    \[ r_X(s,t)=\Cov(X(t),S(t))=\int_0^t\sigma^2txdx+\int_0^s\sigma^2sxdx=\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\Big(t^3+s^3\Big) \]
\end{enumerate}
