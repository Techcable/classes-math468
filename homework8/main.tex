        Covariance is $r_X(s,t)=\Cov(s,t)=E\big[(X(s)-\mu_X(s))(X(t)-\mu_X(t))\big]$. 
        However, we know that $V(X+Y)=V(X)+V(Y)+2\Cov(X,Y)$ and $V(-X)=V(-X)$. I claim that $\Cov(X,-Y)=-\Cov(X,Y)$. By definition of $\Cov$ and using        \[ Cov(X,-Y)=E[(X-E[X])(-1)(Y-E[Y])] \]
        Linearity of expectation allows us to factor out the $-1$, giving the desired identity $\Cov(X,-Y)=-\Cov(X,Y)$. \\
        Hence $V(X-Y)=V(X)+V(Y)+-2\Cov(X,Y)$ and then $\Cov(X,Y)=1/2\big(V[X]+V[Y]-V[X-Y]\big)$.
        In our case we get:
        \[ \Cov(X(t),X(s))=1/2\Big(V[X(t)]+V[X(s)]-V[X(t)-X(s)]\Big) \]
        Since $X(t)\sim Pos_{t\lambda},X(s)-X(t)\sim Pos_{(t\lambda}$, we know that $V[X(t)]=t\lambda$ and $V[X(s)-X(t)]=\lambda(s-t)$. Substituting gives
        \[ \Cov(X(t),X(s))=\frac{\lambda}{2}(t+s-(t-s))=\lambda s \]
        This means that $r_X(t,s)=\lambda s$. \\The symmetry in the answer is somewhat surprising, but Dr. Pickrell says it's fine and comes from our assumption $t>s$. \\
